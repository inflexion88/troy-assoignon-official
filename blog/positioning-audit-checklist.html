Positioning Audit Checklist: 23-Point Self-Assessment

By Troy Assoignon | Published: January 15, 2025 | 15 min read

Quick Assessment Overview

This 23-point audit measures positioning strength across four dimensions:

  1. Strategic Clarity (6 questions): Do you have clear, documented positioning?
  2. Competitive Differentiation (6 questions): Are you meaningfully different from competitors?
  3. Proof & Evidence (5 questions): Can you substantiate your positioning claims?
  4. Market Impact (6 questions): Is your positioning delivering business results?

Scoring: Answer each question with 0 (No), 1 (Somewhat), 2 (Mostly), or 3 (Definitely Yes). Total score out of 69 points.

Interpretation:

  • 55-69: Strong positioning (minor refinements)
  • 35-54: Moderate positioning (strategic updates needed)
  • 0-34: Weak positioning (comprehensive repositioning required)

Most companies operate with weak or undifferentiated positioning—and don't realize it until they're trapped in price-based competition with eroding margins.

This 23-point audit helps you diagnose your positioning strength before it becomes a revenue problem. Use it to:

  • Identify strategic gaps in your positioning
  • Benchmark against positioning best practices
  • Prioritize repositioning initiatives by impact
  • Determine if you need professional positioning diagnostics

Time required: 20-30 minutes for honest self-assessment

How to Use This Audit

Scoring System

For each question, assign a score:

  • 0 = No / Not at all – This doesn't describe us
  • 1 = Somewhat – We partially meet this criterion
  • 2 = Mostly – We largely meet this, with minor gaps
  • 3 = Definitely Yes – We fully meet this criterion

Be brutally honest. Grade yourself as a tough buyer would, not how you want to be perceived.

What to Do With Your Score

  • Track scores by section to identify specific weakness areas (clarity, differentiation, proof, or impact)
  • Prioritize low-scoring items with highest revenue impact first
  • Reassess quarterly to track positioning improvement over time

Section 1: Strategic Clarity (18 points possible)

Strong positioning starts with clarity: you must know exactly who you serve, what you stand for, and how you're different. Without strategic clarity, everything downstream—messaging, sales, marketing—becomes muddled.

Question 1: Can you clearly define your target audience?

What to assess: Do you have a specific, narrow target audience beyond "all businesses" or "mid-market companies"?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: "We serve Series A-C growth-stage B2B SaaS companies in financial services facing regulatory complexity" (highly specific)
  • 2 points: "We serve B2B SaaS companies in growth stage" (somewhat specific)
  • 1 point: "We serve B2B companies" (broad but not meaningless)
  • 0 points: "We serve all businesses" (no targeting)

Why it matters: Narrow targeting enables category ownership. Broad targeting prevents differentiation.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 2: Can you articulate your value proposition in one sentence?

What to assess: Can you clearly state what value you deliver and to whom, in a single sentence that's understandable to buyers?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: One clear sentence that specifies who you serve, what problem you solve, and how you're uniquely positioned to solve it (e.g., "We help growth-stage B2B services companies achieve premium pricing through investor-ready strategic positioning")
  • 2 points: One sentence that captures what you do, but lacks specificity on who or how
  • 1 point: A sentence exists, but it's jargon-heavy or unclear
  • 0 points: No clear value proposition, or it takes multiple sentences to explain

Test: Can a smart 12-year-old understand what you do from your one-sentence value prop?

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 3: Do you own a specific category or position in the market?

What to assess: When buyers think of your category, do they think of you? Or are you one of many undifferentiated options?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: You own a defined category where you're the clear leader (e.g., "positioning expert for growth-stage B2B," "M&A integration specialist for PE-backed industrials")
  • 2 points: You have a category you compete in, and you're recognized as a top player but not the default choice
  • 1 point: You're in a category but not differentiated within it
  • 0 points: You compete in generic categories ("consulting," "agency," "services") with no differentiation

Test: If a buyer Googles your category + a descriptor, do you appear on page 1?

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 4: Do you have a documented positioning statement?

What to assess: Is your positioning formally documented (not just "in people's heads"), and does it include target audience, competitive alternative, unique differentiation, and value delivered?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Formal positioning statement documented and shared across leadership, sales, and marketing with all four components: (1) Target audience, (2) Competitive frame of reference, (3) Unique differentiation, (4) Value delivered
  • 2 points: Positioning is documented but missing one component or not widely shared
  • 1 point: Informal positioning exists (e.g., founder can articulate it) but not documented
  • 0 points: No formal positioning statement

Why it matters: Undocumented positioning creates inconsistency across teams and messaging drift over time.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 5: Does your entire team understand and align on your positioning?

What to assess: If you asked 5 employees "What makes us different from competitors?" would they give consistent answers?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: 100% of sales/marketing/leadership can articulate positioning consistently using the same language and differentiation points
  • 2 points: Most team members (70%+) can articulate positioning, though wording varies
  • 1 point: Some team members (30-70%) understand positioning, but answers are inconsistent
  • 0 points: Team members give wildly different answers about what makes you different

Test: Actually ask 5 team members this question and compare answers.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 6: Can your sales team explain why a customer should choose you (without mentioning price)?

What to assess: Does your sales team lead with strategic differentiation, or do they default to features, capabilities, or discounting?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Sales consistently leads with strategic positioning and value differentiation; pricing is anchored to value, not discounted to win deals
  • 2 points: Sales can articulate differentiation when asked, but often falls back to features or capabilities
  • 1 point: Sales mentions differentiation but relies heavily on price/terms to close
  • 0 points: Sales competes primarily on price or relationship; differentiation is unclear

Why it matters: If your sales team can't sell on differentiation, your positioning is either weak or poorly communicated.

Section 2: Competitive Differentiation (18 points possible)

Positioning is fundamentally competitive—it's about owning territory that competitors don't own. This section assesses whether you have defensible, meaningful differentiation.

Question 7: Do you deliver value that competitors can't easily replicate?

What to assess: Is your differentiation based on proprietary capabilities, specialized expertise, or unique assets? Or could competitors copy you in 6 months?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Your differentiation is based on proprietary methodologies, deep specialization, unique data/assets, or network effects that take years to build
  • 2 points: You have differentiation that's somewhat difficult to copy but not impossible
  • 1 point: Your differentiation exists but is easily replicable (e.g., "we have great customer service")
  • 0 points: No meaningful differentiation; you do what everyone else does

Test: Could a well-funded competitor replicate your differentiation in under 12 months? If yes, it's not defensible.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 8: When buyers compare you to competitors, is the difference clear?

What to assess: Do buyers see you as meaningfully different, or do they lump you in with "similar" alternatives?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Buyers consistently say "you're the only one who does X" or "you're in a different category than the others"
  • 2 points: Buyers acknowledge differences, but still compare you to 2-3 alternatives
  • 1 point: Buyers see minor differences but consider you "basically similar" to competitors
  • 0 points: Buyers say "you all seem the same" or "I'm evaluating 5 similar firms"

Source of truth: Buyer feedback during discovery calls or post-win interviews.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 9: Have you created meaningful switching costs or lock-in?

What to assess: Once a customer works with you, is it difficult for them to switch to a competitor? Or are you easily replaceable?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: High switching costs due to proprietary processes, integration depth, or network effects (customers would lose significant value by switching)
  • 2 points: Moderate switching costs; it would be inconvenient but not costly to switch
  • 1 point: Low switching costs; customers could replace you relatively easily
  • 0 points: No switching costs; you're a commodity service easily replaced

Why it matters: Switching costs = defensible positioning. No switching costs = you compete on price every renewal.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 10: Do you track win/loss analysis to understand competitive dynamics?

What to assess: Do you systematically analyze why you win and lose deals, and use that data to refine positioning?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Formal win/loss analysis for all significant deals, with documented patterns on positioning strength/weaknesses
  • 2 points: Informal win/loss tracking; you know generally why you win/lose but not systematically documented
  • 1 point: Occasional win/loss analysis, but not consistent
  • 0 points: No win/loss analysis; you don't know why buyers choose competitors

Key questions to ask: "Why did you choose us?" (wins) and "What made you choose the alternative?" (losses)

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 11: Do you own "whitespace" territory that competitors haven't claimed?

What to assess: Have you identified and claimed a valuable market position that competitors aren't targeting?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: You've identified and claimed whitespace that's strategically valuable but unclaimed by competitors (e.g., "investor-ready positioning for growth-stage B2B services")
  • 2 points: You compete in a position that has some differentiation but others are moving into it
  • 1 point: Your position has minor whitespace elements but largely overlaps with competitors
  • 0 points: You compete in saturated territory with no whitespace

Test: Search "[your positioning] expert" and see if competitors appear. If page 1 is all competitors, you're in saturated territory.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 12: Do you have a "moat"—a defensible competitive advantage?

What to assess: What prevents competitors from displacing you? Brand equity? Proprietary assets? Network effects? Specialized expertise?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Multiple moats that compound (e.g., proprietary methodology + specialized expertise + category leadership + network effects)
  • 2 points: One clear moat that provides meaningful protection
  • 1 point: Weak moats that provide minimal protection (e.g., "we've been around longer")
  • 0 points: No moats; competitors can replicate everything you do

Common moats: Proprietary frameworks, specialized expertise (years to build), category ownership, brand equity, customer lock-in, network effects, unique data/assets.

Section 3: Proof & Evidence (15 points possible)

Positioning claims without proof = buyer skepticism. This section assesses whether you can substantiate your strategic position with documented evidence.

Question 13: Do you have case studies with documented, quantified results?

What to assess: Can you show buyers concrete proof that your positioning delivers results?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: 5+ documented case studies with specific metrics ($X revenue, Y% margin improvement, Z% efficiency gain) and client attribution
  • 2 points: 2-4 case studies with some metrics but incomplete documentation
  • 1 point: Generic "success stories" without specific metrics or attribution
  • 0 points: No case studies or documented results

Why it matters: "We're great" is a claim. "$4.5M raised for client X in 90 days" is proof.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 14: Can you quantify the ROI of working with you?

What to assess: Do you know—and can you articulate—the financial return clients get from your services?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Documented ROI across multiple clients with specific timeframes (e.g., "average 10x ROI within 12 months, based on margin expansion and pricing improvement")
  • 2 points: ROI data for some clients, not systematically tracked
  • 1 point: General sense of ROI but no specific documentation
  • 0 points: Cannot quantify ROI; value is "strategic" or "intangible"

Test: Can you fill in this sentence with real numbers? "Our clients typically achieve $____ in [revenue/margin/cost savings] within [timeframe]."

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 15: Do you have client testimonials that speak to your positioning?

What to assess: Do testimonials reinforce your strategic position, or are they generic praise?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Testimonials specifically reference your positioning (e.g., "Troy's positioning expertise helped us command 40% premium pricing" vs. "Great to work with!")
  • 2 points: Testimonials mention outcomes but don't tie directly to positioning
  • 1 point: Generic testimonials ("professional," "responsive," "knowledgeable")
  • 0 points: No testimonials or only anonymous quotes

Positioning-specific testimonial example: "Troy's investor-ready positioning framework was instrumental in our $4.5M raise. His strategic clarity turned abstract expertise into fundable narrative."

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 16: Do you demonstrate specialization through content or thought leadership?

What to assess: Have you published frameworks, methodologies, or insights that demonstrate your positioning expertise?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Published frameworks/methodologies, regular thought leadership, speaking engagements, recognized as a category expert
  • 2 points: Some content published, occasional thought leadership, building authority
  • 1 point: Generic blog posts or marketing content, no unique frameworks
  • 0 points: No thought leadership or published expertise

Examples: Named framework (e.g., Strategic Positioning Advantage™), published articles, workshop facilitation, conference talks.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 17: Do you have third-party validation or credibility indicators?

What to assess: Beyond your own claims, do external sources validate your positioning?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Multiple third-party validations: media mentions, industry awards, analyst recognition, client logos from recognized brands
  • 2 points: Some third-party validation (client logos, occasional media mentions)
  • 1 point: Minimal third-party validation
  • 0 points: No external validation; only self-asserted expertise

Why it matters: "I'm an expert" is a claim. "Featured in X publication, worked with Y Fortune 500 companies" is validation.

Section 4: Market Impact (18 points possible)

The ultimate test: is your positioning delivering business results? Strong positioning shows up in pricing power, margins, sales efficiency, and category leadership.

Question 18: Can you command premium pricing vs. generalist competitors?

What to assess: Do you charge 15-30%+ more than less-differentiated alternatives?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: You consistently price 25%+ above generalist competitors and win deals at that premium
  • 2 points: You price 10-25% above competitors and occasionally win at premium pricing
  • 1 point: Your pricing is roughly equivalent to competitors
  • 0 points: You discount to win deals or compete primarily on price

Test: Compare your average deal size to competitors. If yours is higher for the same scope, you have pricing power.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 19: Are your profit margins expanding (or maintaining premium levels)?

What to assess: Do you maintain 30%+ margins, or are you closer to 10-15% commodity margins?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Gross margins of 35%+ and growing (or maintaining premium levels)
  • 2 points: Margins of 25-35%, stable but not expanding
  • 1 point: Margins of 15-25%, under pressure
  • 0 points: Margins below 15% or declining year-over-year

Why it matters: Strong positioning = value-based pricing = premium margins. Weak positioning = price competition = margin compression.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 20: Is your sales cycle efficient (shorter than 6 months for complex B2B)?

What to assess: Does clear positioning accelerate buyer decisions, or do deals drag on due to competitive evaluation?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Sales cycles are 30-50% shorter than industry average; buyers make fast decisions because differentiation is clear
  • 2 points: Sales cycles are average for your industry
  • 1 point: Sales cycles are longer than average due to competitive evaluation
  • 0 points: Extended sales cycles (6+ months for deals that should close faster) with heavy negotiation

Positioning impact: When you're the only option (strong positioning), cycles shorten. When you're one of five (weak positioning), cycles extend.

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 21: Do you attract high-quality inbound inquiries (not just volume)?

What to assess: Are inbound leads pre-qualified by your positioning, or do you get generic "how much does it cost?" inquiries?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Inbound leads are pre-qualified, understand your positioning, and have higher close rates than outbound
  • 2 points: Inbound leads are somewhat qualified but require significant education
  • 1 point: Inbound leads are mostly price shoppers with no positioning understanding
  • 0 points: No meaningful inbound; you rely entirely on outbound prospecting

Test: What percentage of inbound leads say "I found you because you specialize in X" vs. "I'm comparing several firms"?

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 22: Are you perceived as the category leader in your positioning niche?

What to assess: When people think of your category, do they think of you first?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: You're the default choice in your category; buyers start with you and only consider alternatives if you decline
  • 2 points: You're one of the top 3 names mentioned in your category
  • 1 point: You're known in your category but not top-of-mind
  • 0 points: Low category awareness; buyers don't know you exist

Test: Survey 10 potential buyers: "Who's the leader in [your category]?" Do they mention you?

Your score (0-3): _____

Question 23: Is revenue growing because of positioning strength (not just market growth)?

What to assess: Can you attribute revenue growth to stronger positioning (higher pricing, better close rates, category leadership)?

Scoring guide:

  • 3 points: Revenue growth is directly attributable to positioning improvements (documented through win rate, pricing, or market share gains)
  • 2 points: Revenue is growing, and positioning contributes but isn't the primary driver
  • 1 point: Revenue growth is flat or growing only due to market expansion, not positioning
  • 0 points: Revenue is declining or stagnant despite market growth (positioning problem)

Why it matters: If you can't tie positioning to revenue, it's not driving business impact.

Score Interpretation & Next Steps

55-69 Points: Strong Positioning (Minor Refinements)

What it means: Your positioning is fundamentally sound. You have clear strategic direction, defensible differentiation, and market impact. Focus on optimization, not overhaul.

Recommended actions:

  • Identify 2-3 lowest-scoring questions and address those specific gaps
  • Strengthen proof points (case studies, ROI documentation, testimonials)
  • Expand thought leadership to reinforce category ownership
  • Consider positioning diagnostics to find optimization opportunities

Timeline: 30-60 days to address refinements

35-54 Points: Moderate Positioning (Strategic Updates Needed)

What it means: You have positioning foundations but significant gaps in differentiation, proof, or market impact. You're vulnerable to competitive displacement or commoditization.

Recommended actions:

  • Conduct formal positioning diagnostics to identify highest-impact improvements
  • Narrow your target audience to enable category ownership
  • Develop documented case studies with quantified results
  • Update positioning statement and align team on differentiation
  • Consider strategic repositioning engagement (60-90 days)

Common weaknesses at this level:

  • Broad targeting ("we serve all companies")
  • Weak competitive differentiation ("we're like X but with better service")
  • Lack of documented proof points
  • Pricing pressure and margin compression

Timeline: 90-120 days for strategic repositioning

0-34 Points: Weak Positioning (Comprehensive Repositioning Required)

What it means: Your positioning is fundamentally weak or nonexistent. You're likely competing on price, struggling with commoditization, and experiencing margin pressure. This is a critical business issue.

Recommended actions:

  • Immediate: Engage comprehensive positioning strategy to define competitive territory and value architecture
  • Conduct competitive research to identify whitespace opportunities
  • Narrow target audience dramatically (3-5x narrower than current)
  • Document value delivered with case studies and ROI metrics
  • Implement positioning across website, sales, and marketing

Warning signs you're likely experiencing:

  • "You all seem the same" from buyers during evaluations
  • Win rate below 30% in competitive situations
  • Frequent price negotiation and discounting to close deals
  • Long sales cycles (6+ months) with heavy RFP processes
  • Margins below 20% and declining

Cost of inaction: Weak positioning leads to 15-25% pricing discounts, 50-70% lower margins vs. specialists, and competitive displacement by differentiated alternatives.

Timeline: 90-120 days for comprehensive repositioning + 60 days for implementation

Prioritizing Positioning Improvements

If Section 1 (Strategic Clarity) is lowest:

Start here. Without strategic clarity, everything else fails. Actions:

  1. Define narrow target audience (industry + stage + use case)
  2. Document positioning statement with all four components
  3. Align team through positioning workshops
  4. Enable sales with differentiation talking points

If Section 2 (Competitive Differentiation) is lowest:

You have clarity but weak differentiation. Actions:

  1. Map competitive landscape to find whitespace
  2. Identify your unique moats (what competitors can't replicate)
  3. Develop proprietary methodology or framework
  4. Create switching costs through integration or specialization

If Section 3 (Proof & Evidence) is lowest:

You have positioning but can't prove it. Actions:

  1. Document 3-5 case studies with specific metrics
  2. Calculate and publish ROI data
  3. Request positioning-specific testimonials from clients
  4. Publish thought leadership (frameworks, articles, workshops)

If Section 4 (Market Impact) is lowest:

Your positioning isn't translating to business results. Actions:

  1. Audit pricing—are you charging premium vs. competitors?
  2. Analyze win/loss data to identify positioning gaps
  3. Track sales cycle length and close rates by positioning strength
  4. Measure inbound lead quality and category awareness
